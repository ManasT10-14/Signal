# NEPQ: Neuro-Emotional Persuasion Questions

*Summary of "The NEPQ Black Book of Questions" by 7th Level (Jeremy Miner)*

---

## What is NEPQ?

NEPQ (Neuro-Emotional Persuasion Questioning) is a sales methodology that replaces traditional "pitch and close" selling with a structured questioning framework. The core premise: **people buy on emotion and justify with logic**. Instead of telling prospects why they should buy, you ask questions that lead them to convince themselves.

Traditional sales: Rep talks 80%, buyer listens 20%.
NEPQ sales: Buyer talks 80%, rep listens and asks questions 20%.

### The Psychology Behind It

- When YOU tell a prospect they have a problem, they resist (defensive mechanism).
- When THEY tell you they have a problem (through your questions), they own it emotionally.
- Internal motivation (self-generated) never fades. External motivation (someone told you) wears off quickly.
- The prospect becomes the one "selling themselves" on changing their situation.

### What NEPQ Questions Do

1. **Gather, analyze, and diagnose** -- uncover problems, causes, and consequences
2. **Involve the other person** -- make them feel part of the process, not a target
3. **Internally persuade** -- trigger internal motivation to change
4. **Put you in control** -- guide the conversation without pushing
5. **Reveal consequences** -- prospect sees what happens if they do nothing
6. **Create value** -- position you as a trusted advisor, not a vendor

## The NEPQ Dialogue Framework

NEPQ follows a specific sequence of question types. Each phase builds on the previous one to move the prospect from awareness to commitment.

### Phase 1: Connecting Questions

**Purpose:** Build rapport, focus on their world.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"Have you found what you're wanting, or are you still looking for...?"* | Opens dialogue without pressure |
| *"What was it about the [topic] that attracted your attention?"* | Gets them talking about their interest |

**Use 1-2 at the start.** Put the focus on them immediately.

### Phase 2: Situation Questions

**Purpose:** Understand their current state. Ask 3-4 max.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"What are you doing now to handle...?"* | Maps current process |
| *"What type of [solution] are you using?"* | Identifies current vendor/tool |
| *"How long have you been doing it this way?"* | Establishes timeline |

### Phase 3: Problem Awareness Questions

**Purpose:** Surface dissatisfaction with their current situation. Ask 3-4.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"Do you like what your [current solution] does?"* | Forces honest assessment |
| *"What do you like/don't like about...?"* | Identifies pain points |
| *"Why do you like/don't like [specific thing they mentioned]?"* | Deepens the pain |
| *"Is there anything else you don't like about...?"* | Uncovers hidden issues |

### Phase 3b: Two Truths Question (Optional)

**Purpose:** Use when they say everything is fine. Gently challenge the status quo.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"It sounds like things are going fairly well for you. Is there anything you would change about ... if you could?"* | Cracks the "everything is fine" facade |
| *"Why would you change that?"* | Gets the emotional reason |
| *"Has that had an impact on you? In what way?"* | Connects problem to personal pain |

### Phase 4: Solution Awareness Questions

**Purpose:** Let them envision the fix. They own the solution idea. Ask 2-3.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"Have you been out there looking for anything that would give you what you're wanting?"* | Tests if they've tried to solve it |
| *"What have you done about changing this, if anything?"* | Reveals failed past attempts |
| *"How do you see your life being different if this was solved?"* | Creates emotional vision |
| *"What's prevented you from making a change in the past?"* | Identifies real blockers |

### Phase 5: Consequence Questions

**Purpose:** Make them feel the cost of inaction. Ask 1-2. This is the emotional peak.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"What if you don't do anything about this problem and your situation gets even worse?"* | Fear of inaction |
| *"What are you going to do if nothing changes for the next 5-10 years?"* | Long-term pain amplification |
| *"Have you considered the possible ramifications of not doing anything?"* | Forces serious reflection |
| *"How much do you think this problem is costing you in lost revenue?"* | Quantifies the pain |

### Phase 6: Qualifying Questions

**Purpose:** Test urgency and importance. Ask 1-2.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"How important is it for you to change your situation and start...?"* | Measures commitment level |
| *"Why is that important to you now though?"* | Anchors urgency to present |
| *"How would that make you feel to do that?"* | Connects to emotional payoff |

### Phase 7: Transition Questions

**Purpose:** Bridge from problem to solution. This is where you introduce your offering.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"Based on what you told me, what we do might actually work for you, because you said [their problem]..."* | Mirrors their own words back |
| *"...and because of that, it's making you feel [their emotion]..."* | Validates their feelings |
| *"This is what we do: [brief solution]. Does that feel like it might be what you're looking for?"* | Soft pitch through a question |

### Phase 8: Committing Questions

**Purpose:** Close without "closing." The prospect commits themselves.

| Example | What It Does |
|---------|-------------|
| *"Do you feel like this could be the answer for you? Why though?"* | They articulate why they should buy |
| *"Do you feel like this is something that will get you where you want to go? Why do you feel like it is?"* | Self-convincing |
| *"Well, I don't have anything else to go over. The next step is we make an arrangement for [your product]..."* | Natural transition to purchase |

## Advanced NEPQ Techniques

### Clarifying/Probing Questions

Used throughout the conversation to deepen understanding and pull out emotion.

- *"How do you mean exactly?"*
- *"Can I ask why?"*
- *"Can you be more specific? Give me an example..."*
- *"What's prompting you to look into changing this?"*
- *"Why so important now?"*

**Key insight:** The word "challenging" is powerful. When a prospect says something is "challenging," it implies frustration, which is an emotion. Asking *"When you say challenging, what do you mean by that?"* opens the emotional floodgate.

### Expanded Probing Questions

Get prospects to expand their answers to bring out more emotion.

- *"Tell me more..."*
- *"Can you walk me through your company's decision making process?"*
- *"Can you give me a specific example so I can understand this better?"*
- *"Can you go over with me the qualities you look for when choosing a company to work with?"*

### Decision Making Questions

Shift the conversation from "lowest price" thinking to "value" thinking.

- *"Can you walk me through the criteria you use when picking a company to solve these issues?"*
- *"Let's suppose several companies all meet your criteria including price. How would you then decide what to do?"*
- *"You had mentioned the most important thing is cost. Can I ask how that compares to actually getting results?"*

### More Consequence Questions (Complex Sales)

For higher-stakes deals where you need to quantify the cost of inaction:

- *"If you don't solve this, what is the impact on your business?"*
- *"Can you achieve your goals without addressing this problem?"*
- *"What type of effects will this have on your business if you don't do anything about this?"*
- *"How much do you think this problem is costing you this year in lost revenue?"*
- *"Can your company achieve its goals without solving this issue?"*

### Budgeting Questions (Complex Selling)

For multi-stakeholder deals:

- *"Could you go over with me your budgetary process?"*
- *"What obstacles would you possibly encounter when trying to get financial approval?"*
- *"Who else besides you would be involved in approving the budget to solve this?"*
- *"How will you get financial approval from the company to solve this issue you have talked about?"*

## The Proposal Process

NEPQ reframes the proposal as a collaborative process, not a pitch.

### Key Principles

1. **Never "present" -- involve.** Traditional presentations trigger defensive mechanisms. Instead, present by asking questions.
2. **Use "Checking for Agreement" questions throughout:** *"Does that make sense?"*, *"Are we on the same page?"*, *"Do you see how that works?"*
3. **Ask Qualifying Questions during proposal:** *"How important is this issue to you?"*, *"What's holding you back?"*, *"Is there anything else you'd like to go over before we finalize?"*

### Handling "Send Me References"

This is often a stall tactic. NEPQ response: acknowledge, then ask what's really behind it.

### Hypothetical Qualifying Phrases

Used to test commitment without pressure:

- *"Let's pretend we could..."*
- *"What if..."*
- *"Just suppose we could..."*
- *"Imagine for a moment..."*

Then follow with: *"What do you feel would happen next?"* or *"Where would we go from there?"*

## Resolving Questions (Handling Complaints/Objections)

When a customer is upset, NEPQ says: **don't argue, ask questions.**

- *"What happened exactly, just so I know?"*
- *"Can you say more about that so I understand?"*
- *"I appreciate you bringing this to my attention. Can you tell me everything you know about what happened?"*
- *"How do you think it got to this point?"*
- *"I apologize that this happened. What would you like to see done at this point so we can correct this for you?"*

**Key principle:** Arguing with an upset customer inflames the situation. Their emotions are facts to them. Your goal is to win the relationship, not the argument.

## How NEPQ Maps to Signal (Application to This Project)

NEPQ and Signal's behavioral intelligence frameworks are deeply complementary. Here's how:

### Direct Framework Alignments

| NEPQ Concept | Signal Framework | How Signal Detects It |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem Awareness Questions** | FW-05: Question Quality | Signal classifies rep questions as diagnostic vs. leading vs. rhetorical. NEPQ problem awareness questions are high-diagnostic. Signal can score whether the rep is asking NEPQ-style probing questions or generic ones. |
| **Consequence Questions** | FW-08/09: Emotional Turning Points | When a rep asks a consequence question ("What happens if you don't fix this?"), the buyer's emotional state shifts. Signal detects these shifts and can identify whether the rep triggered them intentionally (NEPQ technique) or accidentally. |
| **Commitment/Qualifying Questions** | FW-02: Commitment Quality + FW-06: Commitment Thermometer | NEPQ qualifying questions ("How important is this to you?") produce genuine commitments. Signal's commitment analysis can distinguish between NEPQ-elicited commitments (strong, self-generated) vs. face-saving commitments (weak, pressured). |
| **Situation Questions** | FW-15: Call Structure Analysis | NEPQ mandates a specific flow: Connect -> Situation -> Problem -> Solution -> Consequence -> Qualify -> Transition -> Commit. Signal's call structure framework can detect whether the rep followed this NEPQ sequence or jumped straight to pitching. |
| **Clarifying/Probing Questions** | FW-01: Unanswered Questions | When a buyer gives a vague answer, NEPQ says probe deeper. Signal detects when the rep failed to probe -- the question went unanswered or got a vague response and the rep moved on instead of clarifying. |
| **Decision Making Questions** | FW-10: Frame Match Score | NEPQ decision-making questions shift the buyer's frame from "lowest price" to "value." Signal's frame match analysis detects whether the rep and buyer are aligned on value vs. price framing. |
| **Handling Objections with Questions** | FW-16/17: Pushback Classification + Objection Response | NEPQ says respond to objections with questions, not arguments. Signal can detect whether the rep used NEPQ-style question responses or traditional "overcome the objection" tactics. |
| **Close through Questions** | FW-11: Close Attempt Analysis | NEPQ's committing questions are soft closes. Signal can identify whether the rep used hard closes ("Sign today for a discount") vs. NEPQ-style self-convincing closes ("Do you feel like this could be the answer for you?"). |

### How Signal Could Implement NEPQ Scoring

**Phase 2 potential framework: NEPQ Compliance Score**

Signal could add a dedicated framework that scores each call against the NEPQ dialogue sequence:

1. **Connecting phase detected?** (Opening 0-2 min)
2. **Situation questions asked?** (3-4 max)
3. **Problem awareness questions asked?** (3-4, digging into pain)
4. **Solution awareness questions asked?** (2-3, letting buyer envision)
5. **Consequence questions asked?** (1-2, emotional peak)
6. **Qualifying questions asked?** (1-2, testing urgency)
7. **Transition done via question?** (Not a pitch)
8. **Commitment asked via question?** (Not a hard close)

Each phase scored 0-100, overall NEPQ compliance as a composite.

### Coaching Enhancement

Currently Signal's coaching recommendations are generic ("Ask more open-ended questions"). With NEPQ integration, coaching becomes specific:

- **Instead of:** "Rep should ask better questions"
- **NEPQ coaching:** "Rep jumped from Situation to Transition without asking Problem Awareness or Consequence questions. The buyer never articulated their own pain. Recommendation: After 'What are you using now?', follow with 'What do you like/don't like about it?' and 'What happens if nothing changes?'"

### Why NEPQ Makes Signal More Valuable

1. **Prescriptive, not just descriptive.** Signal currently says "Here's what happened." NEPQ gives Signal a model for "Here's what SHOULD have happened."
2. **Methodology-agnostic scoring becomes methodology-specific.** Instead of generic "question quality," Signal can score against a proven framework (NEPQ, SPIN, Challenger, MEDDIC -- FW-14 already supports this).
3. **Rep training acceleration.** New reps can paste their calls into Signal and get immediate feedback on their NEPQ execution with specific examples of where they deviated and what to say instead.
4. **Manager leverage.** Instead of listening to calls manually, managers see an NEPQ compliance score per rep, per call, with the exact moments where the rep went off-script highlighted in the transcript.
